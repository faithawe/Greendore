Central principle

The problem

Urban dwellers want to grow food. They stop because they don't know when they've failed.

The global indoor farming market is growing rapidly — but adoption is held back not by hardware, but by anxiety. Most smart farming apps present data dashboards to people who have never grown a plant. The result: users who panic at normal slow-growth periods and abandon their pods within 3 weeks.

Greendore is a smart indoor farming pod with a connected mobile app. The design challenge was not "how do we display sensor data" — it was "how do we make a complete beginner feel like an expert grower from the moment they open the app?"

01 — Research

What we learned from real beginner growers

Guerrilla interviews with 14 participants across 3 user segments. Ages 22–41. Urban apartments. Mixed experience levels.

01

The silence is the problem

Beginners don't fear bad news — they fear no news. When nothing happens for 3 days, they assume they've failed. The app needs to narrate normal silence.

"I had no idea if it was working. I assumed I'd killed it."

02

Data creates anxiety, not confidence

Showing 7 sensor readings to a beginner doesn't inform them — it overwhelms them. Users fixated on single numbers and made unnecessary adjustments.

"I kept checking the humidity. I didn't even know what 67% meant for basil."

03

They want a guide, not an alert system

Every participant described their ideal app as "something that would tell me what's happening, like a knowledgeable friend." Not notifications. Guidance.

"I just want someone to tell me if everything is okay."

04

Naming creates ownership

When participants named their test plant during a prototype session, they checked the app 2.3× more frequently in the following 24 hours.

"Once I called her Vesper, I actually felt responsible for her."

05

The harvest is the destination

Users who understood they were growing toward something edible were significantly more patient with slow growth. This connection was missing from all competitor apps.

"I didn't realise I'd be making actual meals from this."

06

Community trust requires verification

General plant community features were dismissed as "just showing off." But pod-verified data — from a real device — was trusted and actively sought.

"If it came from someone's actual pod, I'd trust that."

05

Screen design

22 screens · 9 sheets · full interaction spec

Key design decisions

Every major decision was driven by one question: does this reduce anxiety or create it?

Three decisions shaped the entire product. Each one had a clear alternative — the reasoning behind the choice matters more than the choice itself.

Plant as hero, not pod as hero

Early concepts led with the hardware. Testing showed users cared about the plant. Every screen was reframed: what is the plant doing, not what is the pod reading.

Botanist voice, not alert language

"Humidity below threshold" vs "Vesper is getting dry." Same data. Completely different emotional response. The copy framework became a design deliverable, not an afterthought.

Pod-verified community only

Generic plant social features failed in research. By restricting community to pod-verified results, trust became a structural product feature — not a moderation challenge.

Name your plant — the retention anchor

Optional screen 06 asks users to name their plant. 94% did it — and every single one returned the next day. One optional input outperformed every engagement mechanic considered.

04 — Key screens

22 screens. One emotional arc.

From first impression to daily ritual — every screen serves a specific emotional moment in the user's relationship with their plant.

01

Onboarding — 7 screens, one emotional arc

The onboarding doesn't explain features — it builds a relationship. By screen 7, the user has connected their pod, chosen a plant, named it, and watched it "wake up." The emotional payoff — "Vesper is in good hands" — is the first moment the app speaks in the botanist voice.

Screens 1–2: Wonder. Dark, atmospheric, no feature list. Pod on a windowsill at night. One sentence from the botanist.

Screen 3: Trust. "What should we call you?" — name only, no password on this step. Minimum friction at maximum vulnerability.

Screen 4: Hardware trust. Pulse rings, orbit dot, radar sweep. The pod feels like it's actively searching — not a spinner.

Screen 6: Retention anchor. Naming the plant produced a 2.3× increase in next-day return rate in usability testing.

02

Home — what's happening right now

Built around one principle: absorb anxiety, return calm. Sensors run constantly in the background. The user sees only what they need to act on. Everything else is quiet context.

Hero zone (340px): Plant illustration + pod + harvest progress. The plant is the visual hero — not LED indicators.

Botanist insight card: First card after hero. Dark canopy green creates visual continuity. Written as a quote from the botanist.

Sensor strip: 5 compact chips. One alert state uses terracotta — never red. Quiet leaf green for everything normal.

Today's care: Priority action separated above a divider from completed tasks. One clear action at a time — never competing CTAs.

05 — Prototype & interaction notes

Motion design is not decoration. It's information.

Every animation in Greendore answers a question the user hasn't asked yet. Here are the four moments where motion carries the most emotional weight.

Pod scanning screen

The scanning animation — designed to read as active search, not passive loading

Four rings ripple outward with a 0.55s stagger — creating a wave, not a pulsing blob. A single orbit dot travels ring 3, giving the eye one thing to follow. A radar sweep rotates behind at the same speed, creating a coherent scanning mechanism. Remove either and it reads as a spinner again.

ring duration: 3.2s linear · stagger: 0.55s each orbit: 4s linear · radius: 124px sweep: 4s linear · 360deg entry: opacity 0→1, 120ms stagger per ring

Pod connection — success state

The connected sequence — 7 events in 1.2 seconds, each earning the next

The connection payoff is choreographed: orbit dot fades → success ring springs with overshoot → LEDs light one by one (200ms stagger) → rings shift to calm breathe → headline changes → CTA slides up last. Each event makes the next feel earned rather than simultaneous. Iterated 6 times before it felt right.

0ms: orbit + sweep fade out 100ms: success ring scale(0→1) spring(.34,1.56,.64,1) 200/400/600ms: LED 1/2/3 on 700ms: rings → breathe 2s ease loop 1000ms: CTA translateY(60→0) spring

Toggle switches

A 3px detail that changes how the entire app feels in the hand

Every toggle uses a spring curve with slight thumb overshoot. The thumb travels 18px but momentarily goes 2px past before settling. This single decision makes settings feel physical and satisfying rather than digital and flat. Paired with a light haptic, it's the detail users notice without knowing why.

track: background .25s ease thumb: transform .25s cubic-bezier(.34,1.56,.64,1) travel: 18px · overshoot: ~2px haptic: UIImpactFeedbackGenerator.light (iOS)

Tab navigation

Why tabs crossfade instead of slide — and why that decision matters

Standard tabs slide, implying spatial arrangement (left-right). Greendore's tabs have no natural spatial order — Garden, Grow, Harvest, Community aren't sequential. A slide would lie about the IA. A crossfade communicates what's true: these are parallel contexts, not a progression.

tab switch: opacity 0→1, 200ms ease-out NO slide — tabs are peers, not spatial re-tap active tab: scroll-to-top 300ms push screens: translateX slide 280ms ease-out
Micro-interactions

Task completion

Checkbox scales 1→1.15×, fills green + check in 140ms. Physical, satisfying. Never instant.

Water alert pulse

Alert card border pulses opacity 0.4→1→0.4 once on screen load. Never loops — avoids anxiety.

Progress bar entry

Width 0%→current on screen load. 600ms ease-out, delayed 400ms after screen renders.

Bottom sheet entry

Slides up 380ms spring(0.4, 0.8). Backdrop dims to rgba(0,0,0,.4) simultaneously.

Sparkline bars

Bars grow from 0 to height. 400ms ease-out, 40ms stagger per bar. Left to right — reads as timeline.

Nav icon tap

Icon scale 1→1.12→1.0, 160ms. Active background fades in 120ms. Simultaneous with crossfade.

07 — Retrospective

What I'd do differently. What I'd never change.

What worked exceptionally well

The botanist voice framework — defining copy rules before screens were drawn prevented 80% of the tone inconsistencies that plague early app versions. It was treated as a design deliverable, not a copywriting afterthought.

Naming the plant — one optional input field outperformed every engagement mechanic considered. Emotional ownership drove retention more than any notification strategy.

Pod-verified community — designing trust into the architecture rather than moderating for it made the Community tab feel fundamentally different from any plant social feature on the market.

The scanning animation — the orbit dot + radar sweep combination reads as active search. Users described it as "it felt like it was looking for my pod" — not like a loading spinner.

What I'd do differently

Test the botanist voice earlier — the copy framework was finalised in week 4 but should have been a week 1 deliverable. Two screens had to be redesigned when we discovered the headline hierarchy didn't support the voice we'd defined.

Empty states needed more attention — what does Harvest look like on day 1? What does Community show in a city with no other pods? These edge cases were addressed late and deserved more intentional design.

Accessibility was deprioritised — the terracotta alert color fails WCAG AA on linen backgrounds (ratio 2.8:1). A darker variant would solve this without breaking the palette's emotional register.

Grow tab sub-navigation — Timeline / Environment / Encyclopedia pills work, but users occasionally expected a back button. A more visible active state indicator would reduce this confusion.

What comes next — V2 priorities

Priority 01

Accessibility pass

Full WCAG AA audit. Revised alert color, increased body text contrast, VoiceOver label audit across all 22 screens.

Priority 02

Android adaptation

Material You translation — navigation gestures, bottom sheet behavior, system font rendering, and back-stack differences.

Priority 03

Multi-pod dashboard

Power users with 2–3 pods need a view spanning all pods without making each plant feel like a data row.

Priority 04

AI insight improvement

The Botanist card currently uses templated copy. An LLM integration could generate truly contextual insights referencing actual sensor patterns.

Priority 05

Offline state design

When pod loses WiFi, stale data shows without clearly communicating this. Dedicated offline states with "last updated" timestamps prevent false alarms.

Priority 06

iOS widget design

A home screen widget showing plant state, today's task, and days to harvest — extending the botanist voice into daily phone-checking habit.
